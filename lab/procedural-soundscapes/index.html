Music Theory

To create soothing soundscapes, it helps to have an understanding why certain sounds are more pleasant than others.

A vibrating object can transmit its vibrations to the ear via differences in air pressure, which is then interpreted by the brain as a sound. If the air pressure at the ear is plotted with respect to time, then the resulting graph forms a rough sine wave:

Wave shape as a function of source

The pressure graph draws out looks similar to sine wave since the sound source creating the vibrations is oscillating in a sine-like motion.

The amplitude of this wave corresponds to the difference in sound pressure. This is interpreted by the brain as the intensity or loudness of the sound. While the frequency of the wave is interpreted as its pitch.

Intervals

The above sound has a singular frequency, and can sound fairly plain. However, another wave can be added to it to make it sound relatively richer and more complex:

Try changing the frequency of the second sound (wave) to twice of the other. Now try it again with one and a half times the other one. Try it using other frequencies too, and notice when they sound pleasant. When the freuqency of the two sounds are in simple ratios, they sound harmonious.

Music theory is based around these ratios, or intervals. A frequency ratio of 2:1 is called a perfect octave. A ratio of 3:2 is a perfect fifth, 4:3 is a perfect fourth, and 5:4 is a major third. The smallest of the intervals is called a minor second or semitone, having a ratio of 16:15. The minor second, along with a few other intervals sound dissonant since they do not have simple frequency ratios, but are still useful for creating tension.

This simple integer ratio interval system is the just intonation tuning system. But because of the way the intervals are defined, it is mathematically impossible to have consistent interval ratios between any other two sounds. Instead, the equal temperament (equally spaced intervals) system is used to minimize the overall deviations in intonation.

Scales

Instead of using frequencies and interval ratios to describe pitches, music theory has twelve letters or notes — A, A#, B, C, C#, D, D#, E, F, F#, G, G# — to describe the pitch of a sound. Usually, the A note describes a pitch with a frequency of 440Hz. The other notes denote a frequency that are interval multiples of the base note.

Sharps and Flats

Sharps (denoted using a #) can also be alternatively be written as flats (denoted with a ♭). Sharps indicate the sharper (higher) pitch variant of a note while flats indicate the flatter (lower) pitch variant of a note. For example, C# and D♭ is the same note. Certain notes do not have a sharp or flat variant; the naming convention is purely arbitrary. So all the notes can also be rewritten as: A, B♭, B, C, D♭, D, E♭, E, F, G♭, G, A♭.

The twelve notes are used to describe pitches upto one octave high. To describe pitches that are beyond this range, the same notes are used — with numbers to differentiate them. A note with one number greater indicates a pitch that is an octave higher than the same note with one number lower. Generally, A4 has a pitch of 440Hz, so A5 is 880Hz and A3 is 220Hz. Other notes follow a similar convention.

From the twelve notes of an octave, a subset can be selected to get a scale. Scales are defined by intervals and a note — by selecting a certain subset of intervals, a distinct ‘mood’ for the sounds can be established. Music in major scales generally sound upbeat, while music in minor scales can sound melancholic. The pentatonic scale has an interval structure with five notes and is common in a lot of energetic rock music.

Similar to a scale, a mode is also an interval structure but without a specific root note.

Interval structure harmony

The interval structure of a scale or mode defines its ‘vibe’. A scale with dissonant intervals can sound jarring and tense, while a scale with a consonant intervals can sound upbeat and bright.

When two notes are played separately in succession, it is called a melodic interval. When the two notes are played in unison, it is called a harmonic interval. And when three or more notes are played simultaneously, it is called a chord. Except power chords (which consist of two notes) are also chords, and arpeggios (notes played separately in succession) can be chords too, so the definition is somewhat loose. Typically, chords are triads — consiting of a root note, a third, and a fifth. The names of chords again depend on the intervals of its constituent notes.

Chords can add more depth and texture to sounds than single-pitch notes. The scale, as well the a progression of chords can be selectively chosen to create sounds for evoking a specific mood.

However, the amplitude, just like the pitch, can also be altered to change the character of a sound. Gradually increasing the amplitude of a sound can create drama and tension. Similarly sounds with gradually decreasing volume can be used to resolve tension. The variation in loudness of sounds is called dynamics.

Dynamics can enhance notes and chords by making them more expressive — by adding another layer of character to the sounds.

Rhythm

Try playing these beats:

The first beat is random, and does not necessarily sound pleasant. The second beat is periodic, and sounds slightly more pleasant but nonetheless sound monotonous and dull. The last beat sounds the most rhythmic. A way to make sounds more cadenced is to use a regular pulse beat, and accentuate certain beats periodically. The number of beats for every accentuated beat is usually three or four.

Even then, the beat will begin to sound repetitive if played long enough. The obvious way to go about it is to introduce variations in the duration of certain notes, while still loosely sticking to the same beat structure. The duration of beats can be altered to be half, twice, or quadruple the duration of a single beat. Or somewhere in between.

Altering the rhythm patterns can make sounds structured and more pleasing, but it can also be used to again set the of mood of the sound. The number of beats per minute can be varied to produce different vibes — beats having a fast tempo can sound energetic, while beats with a slow tempo can sound more relaxed and mellow.

Procedural Generation

Music theory provides guidelines for the pitch and timing of notes, to create sounds that evoke different emotions. These can be distilled to:

  • The pitch is derived using structured intervals, or a predefined scale/mode.
  • The timing is derived from a structured beat, with slight variations.

The pitch for the notes can be simply selected from a scale, or it also be dynamically generated easily. However, generating a sequence for the timing that is both structured and non-repeating is slightly more complicated.
